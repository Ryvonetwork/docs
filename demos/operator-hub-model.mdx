---
title: "Operator Hub Model"
description: "How Agon can support hub-style payment operators without turning the base protocol into a single managed rail."
---

Agon can support a **hub model** on top of the base protocol.

In this setup, users open payment channels to a hub, providers open payment channels with that same hub, and the hub handles payment flow between both sides. The hub can enforce its own policy, pricing, compliance, and routing logic, while settlement still happens through Agon's non-custodial protocol state.

## The Basic Shape

Instead of every user opening a direct channel to every provider, the graph can look like this:

```text
user -> hub
hub -> provider
```

The hub becomes the party that:

1. receives signed payment updates from users
2. decides whether to honor or reject a request
3. serves the request directly or forwards it to a provider
4. signs outbound payment updates to providers
5. settles both sides later through normal Agon settlement

## Why This Fits Agon

Agon does not currently do Lightning-style liquidity routing. It does not have path-based forwarding, HTLCs, or atomic multi-hop settlement.

What it does have is exactly what a trusted operator hub needs:

1. permanent participant identities
2. permanent one-way payment channels
3. cumulative commitments
4. payee-side bundle settlement
5. cooperative clearing when several participants want to co-sign one shared update

That means a hub can be built **without changing the base protocol**.

## Example Flow

Imagine Alice wants to pay an RPC provider through a hub.

1. Alice has a channel to the hub.
2. The provider has a channel from the hub.
3. Alice signs a cumulative commitment increasing what she owes the hub.
4. The hub checks Alice's available balance, channel lock, policy rules, and rate limits.
5. The hub serves or forwards the RPC call.
6. The hub signs a cumulative commitment increasing what it owes the provider.
7. Later, the hub settles inbound and outbound channels through normal Agon settlement.

In other words, the hub is doing payment coordination, but the balances still live in protocol state.

## Why This Is Different From A Single Managed Rail

This model can look similar to Circle-style nanopayment infrastructure at the product level:

- one operator coordinates payment flow
- one operator can gate access or apply policy
- users and providers do not need a full mesh of direct payment channels

The difference is that Agon does not require one global operator.

Any team can build a hub on top of the protocol. Users and providers are not locked into one payment company to get access to the settlement rail itself. They can choose different hubs, use direct channels, or mix both models.

## Trust And Liquidity

This model can still be non-custodial, because balances remain in protocol state instead of an operator database.

It can also be fairly trust-minimized if the hub requires enough prefunded or locked capital from the side that is spending. In that setup, the hub is not inventing liquidity out of thin air. It is coordinating payment flow on top of balances and channels that already exist in the protocol.

That said, the hub still has policy power. It can:

1. deny service
2. delay forwarding
3. require additional lockup
4. choose which providers it will work with

That is not a protocol bug. It is the business model of the hub.

## Why A Hub May Still Be Worth It

A hub can make the system much easier to use:

1. users integrate once
2. providers integrate once
3. the hub handles pricing, retries, batching, and monitoring
4. the protocol still gives a shared non-custodial settlement layer underneath

For many real machine-payment networks, that is a much more practical starting point than trying to make every participant open direct channels to every other participant.

## Relationship To `authorized_settler`

`authorized_settler` can help a hub with operational workflows such as relaying or settlement automation, but it is not the main reason the hub model works.

The main reason the hub model works is simpler:

- users owe the hub on one set of channels
- the hub owes providers on another set of channels

`authorized_settler` is useful around the edges. The channel graph is the core of the model.
