---
title: "Yield-Bearing Stablecoins"
description: "How yield-accruing dollar assets fit into Agon, and which token designs work best."
---

Agon can settle in a plain stablecoin like USDC, but it can also settle in a yield-accruing dollar asset.

That matters because Agon is built around deferred settlement. Balances can sit inside the protocol between deposits, lane locks, and later settlement. If the settlement asset keeps earning while it sits there, users and providers do not need to move funds in and out of a separate savings product just to keep idle capital productive.

## The Best Fit For Agon

The best fit is a token whose balance stays fixed while its redemption value rises over time.

On Solana, the important property is simple: you keep the same number of tokens, but each token is worth more of the underlying asset later. Assets like Maple's `syrupUSDC` point in that direction. That design fits Agon's accounting model well because the protocol already tracks balances in fixed token units.

## Why Rebasing Assets Are A Worse Fit

Agon V4 keeps participant balances and lane locks in fixed token amounts inside protocol state.

If the token itself rebases and changes balances underneath the protocol, then the vault's token balance can drift away from the protocol's internal ledger unless the program adds explicit support for that behavior.

So the right target for Agon is not "any yield stablecoin." It is a fixed-balance yield asset.

On Solana, Token-2022 interest-bearing mints are conceptually closer to this fixed-balance model because the stored token balance stays unchanged and the accrued value is calculated separately. Agon V4 does not support Token-2022 today, though, so that would still require token-interface work before it could be used as a first-class settlement asset.

## Why This Is More Interesting In Agon

Yield-bearing stablecoins are useful in many places. They become especially interesting in Agon for three reasons.

First, idle balances do not need to leave the payment system to stay productive. A user or operator can keep working capital inside the protocol instead of moving it back and forth between a payments balance and a separate savings product.

Second, deferred settlement means the system does not pay a full onchain settlement cost on every tiny interaction. That leaves more room for the yield to matter economically.

Third, providers and hubs can keep the same asset on both sides of their business. They can receive payments, keep float, and settle later without switching in and out of a different instrument.

## Who Owns The Yield

This is where the trust model matters.

Agon commitments are denominated in the token itself, not in the underlying base asset. If Alice signs a commitment for `0.01 yUSDG`, she is promising `0.01` shares, not a fixed amount of USDG.

In a fully locked trustless channel, Bob has an enforceable claim to those shares as soon as he receives a valid commitment, as long as he settles before Alice can release the locked funds. In that case, it is fair to think of the future yield on those shares as Bob's from that point onward.

In a trust-minimized relationship, Bob still has a signed claim, but not the same enforceable position. If settlement happens later, Bob still receives the same number of shares, and those shares still carry whatever value they have at that time. The difference is that Bob took timing and counterparty risk between signature and settlement.

So the right statement is:

- in trustless lanes, the payee can treat the committed shares as economically theirs once they can enforce settlement
- in trust-minimized lanes, the payee has a signed claim to those shares, but not the same guarantee until settlement happens

## Pricing Needs One Extra Step

If your business wants to price in dollars while settling in a yield-bearing share token, you need to convert the quote into shares at the moment the commitment is signed.

Otherwise, a fixed share amount slowly becomes more expensive in dollar terms as the exchange rate rises.

```ts
const quoteInUsd = 0.01;
const exchangeRate = 1.0245; // 1 yUSDG currently redeems for 1.0245 USDG
const shareAmount = quoteInUsd / exchangeRate; // 0.00976086 yUSDG
```

That is not a protocol problem. It is just the normal pricing rule for any appreciating share token.

## What The Yield Math Really Looks Like

A headline APY can make the upside sound larger than it really is, so it helps to think in average balances.

At `6%` APY:

- an average balance of `$1 billion` for a full year produces about `$60 million` of gross yield
- an average balance of `$1 million` for a full month produces about `$5,000` of gross yield

But if a merchant earns `$1 million` gradually over the course of a month and withdraws it at the end, the average balance during that month is closer to `$500,000`, not `$1 million`. In that more realistic case, the gross monthly yield is closer to `$2,500`.

That does not make the idea weak. It just means the right metric is average balance kept in the system, not top-line revenue alone.

## Where This Is Going

The broad pattern already exists. Solana already has fixed-balance yield assets such as Maple's `syrupUSDC`, and more products in that direction will likely appear as stablecoin issuers compete on payments, savings, and treasury use cases.

What Agon adds is not the yield-bearing asset by itself. The interesting part is combining that asset with:

- long-lived payment lanes
- signed cumulative commitments
- deferred settlement
- bundle settlement
- cooperative clearing

That makes yield-bearing balances part of the payment rail itself instead of a separate savings layer bolted on the side.

## The Main Constraints

| Constraint | Why it matters |
| --- | --- |
| Pricing | If your product is priced in dollars, you need a share-to-dollar conversion step at quote time |
| Issuer rules | Some products have KYC, geography limits, cooldowns, or redemption fees |
| Token design | Rebasing assets are a poor fit for Agon's current accounting; fixed-balance assets fit better |
| Token program support | Agon V4 currently uses the original SPL Token program, not Token-2022 |
| Trust model | Yield does not remove counterparty risk; it just makes deferred settlement more useful when the relationship already works |

## What This Means In Practice

Think of a yield-bearing settlement asset as a way to make payment float productive.

Agon does not create the yield. The token design does that. Agon makes it practical to keep that asset inside a machine payment system without forcing every interaction to settle immediately.

That is the practical reason this asset design fits Agon so well.
